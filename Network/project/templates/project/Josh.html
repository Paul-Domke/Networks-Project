Denial of Service Attacks

Joshua Ingram

Overview

Have you ever been in a situation where you're browsing a website, minding your own business, and your experience seems to come to a grinding hault after being met wit the infamous loading symbol? Sometimes this could be due to a slow internet connection, but if this experience goes on for hours at a time and does not improve, this could be a result of a Denial of Service (DOS) attack.

These DOS attacks overwhelm a webserver by flooding its connections with abnormally large amounts of web traffic. This attack results in the server being unable to communicate with normal users, causing them to be "denied the service" of using the website. People that perform these attacs could be politcally or socially motivated, motivated by money or resentment towards a specific company, or could be mischievous individuals with no greater intent than creating chaos.

Denial of Service vs Distributed Denial of Service

A DOS attack only involves a single end system that is the attackers computer and the connection to the server. This attack still has the intent of overwhelming the server's bandwidth and resources, but is often on a smaller scale. Some damage can be done, but with modern, large servers hosted by companies like Sony, a single system attacking the server is usually not enough to create a largely noticeable effect.

While this single end system is attacking a server, the server can generally pick out the connection being used to perform this attack by seeing the flood of packets. It can then halt this connection and prevent the attack. However, with this shortcoming of DOS attacks comes an alternative: Distributed Denail of Service (DDOS) attacks. A DDOS attack involves multiple end systems that simultaneously flood a server, drastically increasing the effects seen in a single DOS attack.

These DDOS attacks are usually orchestrated by an attacker or team of attackers by infecting thousands, if not millions, of individuals' computers using malware. Once this malware is on the collection of infected computers, also known as a botnet, a DDOS attack can be planned and executed by controlling the software to flood the chosen server using the thousands of devices with the malware. The effects of a DDOS attac amplifies that of the DOS attack because there are even greater amounts of end systems being used; resulting in even the largest and most complex servers to be vulnerable to these attacks.

Flood Attacks

There are a number of different approaches to DOS attacks, but "flooding" a server and taking up its bandwidth is the most common of ways. One of the most common attacks that takes advantage of internet protocols is known as a SYN flood.

SYN Flood

A SYN flood takes advantage of the three-way "handshake" in TCP through the SYN, SYN-ACK, and ACK messages. For simplicity, we will use a single attacking end system to illustrate how it works. In a SYN flood attack the attacker will send repeated SYN messages to its target of interest to all of its target's ports. These SYN messages will be responded to by the target server with SYN-ACK messages from each port. The attacker will not respond to the SYN-ACK message with the usual ACK message, so the connection will be left open. Just before the connection times out, another SYN packet will be sent and this will leave the connection open, increasing the amount of connections being taken up and real users will be denied the use of the webserver because the server will have no open ports or will malfunction.

Summary

Denial of Service attacks have been around for years now, creating negative outcomes for the server hosts and the users trying to use the webservers' services. There have been a number of well known take downs of servers due to these types of attacks, some very recently. Although the attacks have been able to become more complex, the organizations running these servers being targeted have started to develop defenses against DOS and DDOS attacks that help prevent them from occuring as often.
